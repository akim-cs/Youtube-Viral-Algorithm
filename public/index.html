Youtube Viral Algorithm

Andy Kim and Ryan Kim

Content creators on youtube have been trying to crack the viral algorithm for years, and we thought it would be interesting to explore how the algorithm works and how it can be used to create viral content.

What goes into a video blowing up? Is it the title, the thumbnail, the content, or some combination of other factors? We will explore these questions.

Understanding Video Timing: Days from Published to Trending

This timeline visualization displays the distribution of time elapsed between a video's publication date and when it appeared on YouTube's trending page. Each mark represents an individual trending video from the dataset, positioned according to how many days passed before achieving trending status. The distribution reveals critical patterns about YouTube's recommendation velocity—whether viral success tends to be immediate or can develop over extended periods—and helps identify outliers that buck typical trending timelines.

The data shows a strong concentration of videos trending within the first 40 days after publication, suggesting YouTube's algorithm prioritizes recent content. However, the presence of videos trending 200+ days after publication indicates that "slow-burn" viral success remains possible, though significantly less common than immediate viral growth. Upon further inspection, we find that videos trending after 200 days often belong to categories such as educational content or commercials.

Posting Frequency vs. View Count Relationship

This scatter plot examines whether the frequency of video uploads correlates with maximum viewership success. The x-axis represents the number of videos a channel published during the dataset (November 2017 - June 2018), displayed on a logarithmic scale to accommodate the wide range from single-upload channels to prolific daily posters. The y-axis shows each channel's highest-performing video view count, also logarithmically scaled. This visualization tests a fundamental content creator hypothesis: does posting more frequently lead to higher peak viewership, or is video success independent of upload volume?

The scatter plot reveals no clear positive correlation between posting frequency and peak viewership—channels across all upload frequencies achieve both high and low view counts, with significant vertical spread at every frequency level. This pattern suggests that content quality, timing, and algorithmic factors may play more decisive roles than sheer upload volume in determining viral success.
